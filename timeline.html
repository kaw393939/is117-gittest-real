Timeline

A brief history of the ideas and moments that shaped a human-centered editorial modernism.

1960s–1970s — The humanist revival

Designers began revisiting letterforms with roots in handwriting and human proportions. The result was a softer modernism: more warmth, more attention to reading comfort, and less reliance on strict geometric rules.

1970s — Adrian Frutiger

Frutiger’s work emphasized legibility and calm presence. His typefaces bridged practical needs and humanist shapes that read easily in many contexts.

1980s–1990s — Spiekermann & Carter

Erik Spiekermann and Matthew Carter advanced type systems suitable for multiple media. Their pragmatic, reader-focused work helped humanist letterforms find a wide audience in print and on screen.

1990s — Rise of UX

Designers began centering users: clarity, direction, and plain language became central. Humanist typography and generous layouts supported the shift toward more empathetic interfaces.

2000s — iA Writer and focus tools

Minimal writing environments emphasized typographic rhythm, whitespace, and focus. These tools popularized the idea that writing interfaces can be calm, typographically honest spaces.

2010s — Accessibility as mainstream

Accessibility moved from niche to essential. The movement’s values — semantic structure, readable type, and inclusive controls — dovetail with Humanist Modernist practice.
